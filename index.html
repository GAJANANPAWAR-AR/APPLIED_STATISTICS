📖 Theoretical foundations & formulas

Mean (average): $$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i$$

Median: Middle value when data sorted. If n even, average of two middle values.

Mode: Most frequent value.

Variance (sample): $$s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}$$

Standard deviation: $$s = \sqrt{s^2}$$

Coefficient of Variation (CV): $$CV = \frac{s}{\bar{x}} \times 100\%$$

Quartiles & IQR: Q1 (25th percentile), Q3 (75th), IQR = Q3 – Q1.

Outlier rule (1.5×IQR): Lower bound = Q1 – 1.5·IQR, Upper = Q3 + 1.5·IQR.

Mean error: Bias = \(\bar{x} - \text{Target}\)

  • Mean = 2.083 mm | Median = 2.1 mm | Mode = 2.1 mm
  • Variance = 0.035, σ = 0.187 mm | CV = 9.0%
  • Quartiles: Q1=1.95, Q3=2.2 → IQR=0.25 mm
  • Outlier bounds (1.5×IQR): [1.575, 2.575] → ✅ no outliers
  • Mean error (target 2.0 mm) = +0.083 mm (negligible bias)
Accuracy vs Repeatability: Accuracy = closeness to true value, Repeatability = variation under same conditions. Our system shows high repeatability (σ=0.187mm) and minimal bias.

Histogram: positional deviation bins [mm]

🏭 Engineering conclusion: stable, symmetric, repeatable process, no outliers → ideal for precision assembly tasks.

🎲 Chapter 2 – Probabilistic Modeling of Uncertainty

📐 Probability axioms & key rules

Sample space (S): set of all outcomes. Event: subset of S.

Axioms: \(P(S)=1\), \(0\le P(E)\le1\), for disjoint events \(P(\bigcup E_i)=\sum P(E_i)\).

Addition rule: \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\).

Multiplication rule (independent): \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\).

Conditional probability: \(P(A|B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}\).

Law of Total Probability: \(P(B)=\sum_i P(A_i)P(B|A_i)\).

Bayes' theorem: \(P(A_i|B)=\frac{P(A_i)P(B|A_i)}{\sum_j P(A_j)P(B|A_j)}\).

Automation example - Total probability: 3 production lines L1(30%, defect 2%), L2(45%,3%), L3(25%,4%) → overall defect rate = \(0.3×0.02 + 0.45×0.03 + 0.25×0.04 = 0.0295\) (2.95%).
Bayesian fault diagnosis: Prior \(P(F)=0.02\), sensor sensitivity \(P(Alarm|F)=0.98\), false alarm \(P(Alarm|\neg F)=0.04\). Then \(P(Alarm)=0.0588\), posterior \(P(F|Alarm)=0.3333\) (33.3%). Even with good sensor, low prior yields moderate posterior → need redundant sensors.
💡 Interpretation: Bayesian updating is crucial for automated inspection systems (vision, vibration) to reduce false alarms and improve reliability.

📈 Chapter 3 – Statistical Distribution Models

📉 Binomial Distribution

PMF: \(P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\). For n=50, p=0.04: Mean=np=2.0, σ=√[np(1-p)]=1.386.

P(X=2)=0.2652, P(X=0)=0.1299, P(X>3)=0.1574

⚡ Poisson Distribution

\(P(X=k)=e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k/k!\). λ=1.0 failures/board. Mean=Variance=1.0.

P(X=0)=0.3679, P(X=2)=0.1839, P(X≥3)=0.0803.

🔔 Normal Distribution

\(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\). pdf: \(f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}\).

Placement deviation \(N(0.50,0.08^2)\). Spec limits [0.30,0.70] → Z=±2.5 → 98.76% within spec. 95th percentile = 0.6316 mm.

Empirical rule: 68% within μ±σ, 95% within μ±2σ, 99.7% within μ±3σ → alarm thresholds. Z-scores: \(Z=(x-\mu)/\sigma\). Gripper Z_A=2.40, Wrist Z_B=2.80 → wrist more unusual.

📐 CLT & Joint Distributions

Central Limit Theorem: \(\bar{X}\sim N(\mu, \sigma/\sqrt{n})\) approx. Cycle time μ=12.0s, σ=1.8s, n=36 → SE=0.30s, \(P(\bar{X}>12.5)=0.0475\) (Z=1.667).

Covariance & Correlation: \(\text{Cov}(X,Y)=\frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{n-1}\), \(r=\frac{\text{Cov}}{s_X s_Y}\).

Paired data (deviation vs temperature): Cov=0.2557, r=0.9996 → almost perfect positive linear relation (thermal expansion).

📐 Chapter 4 – Statistical Inference for Automation Decisions

Confidence intervals & Hypothesis testing formulas

Z-interval (σ known): \(\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)

t-interval (σ unknown): \(\bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\)

CI for proportion: \(\hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\)

Hypothesis testing steps: H₀, H₁, test statistic, critical value/p-value, decision.

t-test statistic: \(t = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}\)

Z-test statistic: \(Z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\)

Type I error (α): reject true H₀ (false alarm). Type II error (β): fail to reject false H₀ (missed detection).

Z-interval (σ known): n=36, \(\bar{x}=12.5\), σ=1.8 → 95% CI \([11.912,13.088]\)
t-interval (σ unknown): n=12, \(\bar{x}=2.08\), s=0.187 → 95% CI \([1.961,2.199]\) mm
CI for proportion: 4/100 defects → 95% CI \([0.0016, 0.0784]\)

📌 Hypothesis Testing Examples

t-test (σ unknown): H₀: μ=2.0 vs H₁: μ≠2.0, n=12, \(\bar{x}=2.083\), s=0.187 → t=1.481, df=11, t_crit=2.201 → fail to reject H₀ (p≈0.166). No significant bias.

Z-test (σ known): σ=0.2, n=30, \(\bar{x}=2.09\) vs μ₀=2.0 → Z=2.46 > 1.96 → reject H₀. Process mean shifted.

⚠️ Type I error (false alarm) may cause unnecessary recalibration; Type II error may allow defective products.

🎯 Statistical vs Practical significance

A small shift may be statistically significant but not practically important if within engineering tolerance. Always combine with domain knowledge.

Confidence interval width comparison: Known σ yields narrower intervals.
